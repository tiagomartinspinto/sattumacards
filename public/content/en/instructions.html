Here are two possible ways to play with the web game cards. The idea is that each card, together with the other cards turned onto the table, defines an imagined teaching situation.

The players' task is to imagine and describe, using the information given by the cards, what kind of teaching situation is taking place and how it might continue or change as the cards change.

The virtual game table and its cards appear in the same way to everyone who has joined the game. As the games become familiar, the rules can also be adapted to the players' needs.

Game A is guided by the interface. Game B is a freer variation that can be played socially with the same table.

For example, players may agree that a card is occasionally or in a certain situation empty or a "joker", allowing the text on the card to be changed into something else.

GAME A

Start a round by choosing one card from each of the six decks. Players can take turns dragging cards onto the table themselves, or press the Sattuma button to deal a random six-card situation automatically.

When there are six cards on the table, turn them over and read them aloud. The players describe together what kind of teaching situation the cards create. The discussion may be timed if the group wants to keep the game moving.

After the discussion, the next player replaces one card on the table with a new card from the same deck. The new card is turned over and read aloud. The players then describe how this change affects the teaching situation.

The game continues by alternating between discussion and single-card replacement. The group may agree in advance how many replacement rounds to play, or continue until the decks run out or the story no longer changes meaningfully.

The Start over button clears the table, shuffles the cards, and returns the game to the beginning of a new round.

GAME B

The game can also be played so that cards are dealt from the six decks onto the game table as before, but all players interpret the turned cards together.

Once the row has been spoken aloud as a story of a teaching event, one player turns a new card and the players again imagine and describe aloud the change caused by it.

Telling the story together always produces several possibilities, and in each round the players must agree what kind of teaching event and shared story is formed by each row of turned cards.
